The Last Garden — Jane Doe
Chapter One

The garden had always been her mother's domain. Even now, three years after the funeral, Eleanor could smell the rosemary when she closed her eyes. The house stood at the edge of the village, its walls thick with ivy that had, in the years of neglect, begun to prise apart the mortar between the old stones.

She knelt beside the overgrown beds, her fingers tracing the stone edging that her mother had laid decades ago. The soil was cold and unyielding beneath her nails, packed hard by seasons of rain and frost and the slow, patient work of roots pushing through from below.

The garden had been magnificent once. She had seen the photographs — her mother at twenty-five, laughing in a sundress, surrounded by banks of delphiniums and hollyhocks that towered above her head. The borders had been so precise you could have drawn them with a ruler, every edge trimmed, every plant staked and tied with raffia in neat bows.

Now the beds were a tangle of couch grass and bindweed. The box hedging, once clipped into neat spheres, had grown into wild, lopsided shapes that reminded Eleanor of creatures in a children's book. The gravel paths had disappeared under a creeping tide of moss and self-seeded forget-me-nots.

She sat back on her heels and looked up at the house. The kitchen window was dark. Inside, the solicitor's letter still lay on the table where she had left it that morning, its implications settling into her understanding like water into dry earth. The house was hers now, and with it the garden, and with the garden came a choice she was not yet ready to make.

"You'll want to get the soil tested before you do anything drastic," said a voice from behind the wall. Eleanor startled. A head appeared above the stone boundary — white-haired, sharp-eyed, unmistakably Margaret Ashworth from the cottage next door.

"I wasn't planning on doing anything drastic," Eleanor said, standing and brushing the dirt from her knees.

"Your mother said the same thing, and then she pulled out every last rose and replaced them with lavender." Margaret sniffed. "Lavender. In clay soil. I told her it wouldn't take, and I was right."
